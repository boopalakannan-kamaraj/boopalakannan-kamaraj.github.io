01Context

Reports that
couldn't talk to each other.

Karnataka State Police's crime reporting was static and siloed — investigators had no way to see hotspots, trends, or suspect networks without manually cross-referencing reports across districts and timeframes.

For the KSP Datathon 2026, the challenge was to design and build an AI-driven crime-analytics platform: district-level drill-down, spatiotemporal hotspot clustering, trend anomaly alerts, criminal network / link analysis, and predictive risk scoring — production-grade in approach, not just a hackathon prototype.

“An investigator should go from a map to a suspect's full case history without leaving the screen.”
02The challenge

Five questions a
dashboard had to answer.

Before building, I mapped what an investigator actually needs to ask of crime data — and each question became one of the five core surfaces.

Fig. 01 · What the platform had to surface KSP Datathon · 2026
Need 01Where

Where is crime concentrating?

No spatial view. Hotspots were invisible without manually plotting reports by hand.
No drill-down. District-level patterns couldn't be opened to the incidents behind them.
Need 02When

What's trending — and abnormal?

No temporal lens. Spikes and seasonal patterns went unnoticed until after the fact.
No alerts. Anomalies weren't flagged, so nothing prompted a closer look.
Need 03Who

How are suspects connected?

No link analysis. Relationships between suspects, cases and locations lived in separate files.
Manual cross-referencing. Building a network meant reading reports side by side.
Need 04What next

Where is risk rising?

No forward view. Resourcing was reactive — there was no predictive risk signal per district.
No single number. Nothing summarised “how hot is this area right now?”
Approach

Each question maps to a surface: Crime Map for where, Trends for when, Network Analysis for who, and Risk Scoring for what's next — tied together by a Dashboard and a natural-language Ask layer.

06Reflections

Make the data
answerable.

The hard part wasn't drawing five dashboards — it was making them hand off cleanly, so an investigator never hits a dead end. The map-to-network drill-through and the Ask layer come from the same instinct: the platform earns trust by getting you from a question to an answer in as few moves as possible.

Building in Cursor under a datathon clock proved the point of an AI-native workflow — I could wire real data shapes the same day a surface was designed, and feel whether a flow held up instead of arguing over a static mock.
